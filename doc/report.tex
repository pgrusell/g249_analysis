This document is a brief description of the different task performed in the analysis of the G249 experiment. In particular we focus on the work done by the macros of the GitHub repository \href{https://github.com/pgrusell/g249_analysis}{g249\_analysis}.

\section{Calibrations}

These macros check the performance of the calibrations of some detectors (FOOTs and NeuLAND for the moment).

\subsection{FOOT}

For FOOTs, all the calibrations were done in-flight by R3BROOT (in map2cal and cal2hit classes), so the only correction done after the experiment was charge calibration. The problem is that there is a dependence on the energy with the eta parameter that does not allow to perform a direct conversion between energy and charge (see \cref{fig:cal_foot_eta} left).

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering

    \begin{subfigure}{0.45\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/cal_foot_no_etaCorr.png}
    \end{subfigure}
    \hfill
    % eta correction
    \begin{subfigure}{0.45\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/cal_foot_etaCorr.png}
    \end{subfigure}

    \caption{Energy as a function of the eta parameter. Left: With eta correction or charge calibration, only one ASIC. Right: After eta correction and charge calibration for all the ASICs of the detector.}
    \label{fig:cal_foot_eta}
\end{figure}

To make the eta correction we perform a high-degree polynomial fit to the $Z=8$ and $Z=9$ bands for each ASIC chip and calibrate the energy by making a linear fit (channels, charge) for those bands. This work is done by R3BROOT and, in the macro \texttt{check\_eta\_correction.C}, we plot the charge as a function of eta to show that the former dependence has disappear (see \cref{fig:cal_foot_eta} right).

On the other hand, this macro also represent the charge measured in the FOOTs (after all the corrections) against the charge measured in LOS. In \cref{fig:cal_foot_ZLosVsFOOT}, we see a clear correlation between both charges. Whereas for charges different from $Z=8$ and $Z=9$ we see some saturation, this is not present for our charges of interest.

\subsection{Neutron emission decay}

Here we simulate the decay of the \textsuperscript{24}O, produced in (p,2p) reaction, by neutron emission. The idea is to use the generator \href{https://github.com/pgrusell/R3BNeutronDecayGenerator}{\texttt{R3BNeutronGenerator}}. There, we will input the mass and charge of the fragment produced in the de-excitation via neutron emission of the incoming fragment, the kinetic energy per nucleon in the lab frame of the fragment, $\sim 650$ MeV, the number of emitted neutrons and the energy and width of the resonance that we want to simulate.

Since in that experiment they were able only to measure one proton during the hole deexcitation process, three possible scenarios are available, \textsuperscript{24}O\textsuperscript{*} decaying via one neutron emission follow by the emssion of a second neutron emission until reaching the \textsuperscript{22}O. Measuring any of the neutron would result in either blue or read peaks of \cref{fig:sim_nndecay_rel_energy}. However, there are still other options, the most obvious one is the simultaneous emission of two neutrons by the \textsuperscript{24}O\textsuperscript{*}, decaying directly to \textsuperscript{22}O without stopping in the resonant blue level and thus, not contributing to the previous resonant peaks. This case is not going to be explored for the moment.

In order to simulate these resonances we will use the neutron generator to explore the following reactions:

\begin{equation}
    \text{ \textcolor{red}{\textsuperscript{24}O}} \longrightarrow \text{ \textcolor{blue}{\textsuperscript{23}O}} + 1n
    \label{eq:sim_nndecay_24O_23On}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \text{ \textcolor{blue}{\textsuperscript{23}O}} \longrightarrow \text{ {\textsuperscript{22}O}}  + 1n
    \label{eq:sim_nndecay_23O_22On}
\end{equation}

From the results of \cite{Hoffman2009_O24}, we know that \cref{eq:sim_nndecay_24O_23On}, corresponds to a resonance of $\sim 0.6$ MeV, while \cref{eq:sim_nndecay_23O_22On}, to $0.045(2)$ MeV. For both cases we can simply consider the kinetic energy of the recoil fragment as the energy of the \textsuperscript{25}F beam ($\sim 650$ MeV/u). Therefore,running the generator with the following configuration:

~

\begin{lstlisting}[style=bash]
$ root -l 'NeutronDecayGenerator.C("O24_O23n", 10000, 23, 8, 650., 0.6, 0.25)' 
$ root -l 'NeutronDecayGenerator.C("O23_O22n", 10000, 22, 8, 650., 0.045, 0.02)'
\end{lstlisting}

This will generate two different \texttt{.out} files that can be separately read by the simulation class to generate the corresponding events. To run proceed in this way we can simply use the messenger \texttt{O24\_2n\_decay.mac}, this will generate one rootfile per resonance that can be analyzed.
