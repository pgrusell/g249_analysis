\section{Cross sections}

The first objective is to calculate the cross section for the population of the \textsuperscript{24}O\textsubscript{g.s}, which follows the equation:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma = -\frac{M_t}{\rho_tN_Az}\ln\left(1-\frac{N_{frag}^{p2p}}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}\right)
    \label{xs_equation}
\end{equation}

Where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $M_t$ is the molar mass of the LH\textsubscript{2} target: $M_t=1.000784$ g/mol.
    \item Density of the LH\textsubscript{2} target: $\rho_t = 0.0715$ g/cm\textsuperscript{3}.
    \item Length of the target: $z=5$ cm.
    \item Avogadro's number: $N_A=6.022\times 10^{23}$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{$N_{proj}$}



$N_{proj}$ represents the number of projectiles that arrive at the target. This number will be calculated from the unreacted beam after the target. This can be done as follows:

$$N_{proj} = \frac{N_{unreacted}}{1 - \text{reaction rate}}$$

Where the reaction rate is given by \href{https://web-docs.gsi.de/~weick/atima/}{ATIMA}. The advantage of this method is that if we count the number of unreacted fragments with the same method as the reacted fragments (MDF, as will be explained later), we don't need to take into account the efficiencies in the detectors before the target.

The reaction rate for an incoming beam of \textsuperscript{25}F impinging onto a $5$ cm LH\textsubscript{2} target is 0.0024. See \cref{xs_atima_file_scheme} for the specific information regarding the materials considered to calculate the reaction rate.

\subsubsection{Incoming selection}

Now we have the reaction rate, the following step is to count the number of \F that didn't react. The first condition that we are going to apply is the selection of the \F as incoming in the FRS/LOS setting. To do this, we apply a rectangular cut in the A/Q (from FRS data) vs Z (from LOS data) plot in the region of the \F. The values used are: $8.2 \leq Z \leq 10$ and $2.77 \leq A/Q \leq 2.79$. The result of the cut is shown in the following figure:

\begin{figure}[H]

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/xs_aoq_vs_Z_cut_incoming.png}
    \caption{A/Q from FRS vs Z from LOS to make the identification of the incoming beam. In red, cut applied to select just \F.}
\end{figure}

The first thing that we notice here is that a better way to select the incoming is clearly needed as this seems not to be the best way possible. Nevertheless, the effect is probably not so big, since it should affect in the same way to the number of projectiles reconstructed from the unreacted and the \textsuperscript{24}O measured, but it's anyway a source of systematic uncertainties.

\subsubsection{Unreacted selection: MDF}
\label{subsub_unreacted_selection}

After applying this cut, we will look for the \F that did not react after the target in the MDF. This MDF, have the following restrictions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Data (the branch itself) of the following detectors has to be present: LOS, FRS (both within some AoQ/Z cut), IncomingFootTrack and OutgoingFootTrack, FootHitData (which is redundant since the outgoing/incoming tracks are reconstructed from it), TofdHitData and Fiber data.

    \item Certain parameters such as glad current, FrsCutMeanZ... have to be set in the macro of the unpacker.

    \item For an event to be considered, it has to have information of FRS, LOS, first plane of ToFD (within a given energy window), foot\_incoming, foot\_outgoing, fib30 (X) and fib32 (Y).

    \item At least one of the fibers 31 and 33 have to have data (since they share plane).

    \item In order to make the tracking (incoming and outgoing) of the FOOTs, following conditions are necessary:

          \begin{itemize}
              \item FootHitData and FRSData.
              \item Just 8 FOOTs in the mapping parameters.
              \item The VFTX number in the FRS data has to be the same as selected\footnote{This is due to the fact that during our experiment a second VFTX was installed in the middle of the beam time. Since it has better resolution and acceptance it is the one that we should use when available.}.
              \item Only the highest energy hit in every detector will be used.
              \item Data in all 8 FOOTs has to be present.
          \end{itemize}

    \item For fibers, only the highest energy hit in every fiber will be used for this reconstruction. In case that there is signal both in Fib31 and Fib33 the one with higher energy will be used.
\end{enumerate}

Under these conditions, the MDF provide information regarding the A/Q and P/Q of the outgoing fragment after the target that, together with the charge in ToFD, will make possible the outgoing frgament identification.

In the first case of interest, we will select the unreacted \F, from the A/Q vs Z plot. As we can see, the unreacted clearly dominates over the rest of the isotopes. As first approximation, we can consider that, for a sufficiently narrow cut around the centroid of the \F ``blop'', the contamination from other isotopes is negligible.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/xs_AoQ_Z_outgoing_25F.png}
    \caption{A/Q (from the MDF) vs Z (from ToFD first plane) with the selection in \F as incoming.}

\end{figure}

In these conditions, to make a systematic selection of the \F we can make a fit to a 2D-Gaussian function:

\begin{equation}
    f(Z, AoQ) = p_0\exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{Z-p_1}{p_2}\right)^2-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{AoQ-p_3}{p_4}\right)^2\right]
\end{equation}

This will provide both centroids of the ``blop'' ($\mu_{AoQ}=2.740$, $\mu_{Z}=8.976$) and the standard deviations ($\sigma_{AoQ} = 0.00988$, $\sigma_{Z}=0.1973$). Thus, a elliptical cut defined in terms of constant number ($k$) of $\sigma$ around the centroid, can be used to select the \F.

Now that we have defined the cut, is important to notice that just counting the number of nuclei inside the cut will bias the result: a higher $k$ will increase the number of isotopes and a smaller cut will reduce it. Hence, is important no estimate the efficiency ($\varepsilon$) of the cut: How many of the real events are inside the cut. One approach could be use the analytical formula of the Gaussian efficiency:

\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon = 1 - e^{-k^2/2}
    \label{gaussian_eff}
\end{equation}

According to this efficiency, the number of real \F can be estimated as the number of counts inside the cut, divided by the efficiency of the cut:

\begin{equation}
    N_{proj} = \frac{N_{proj}^{(ROI)}}{\varepsilon}
\end{equation}

Using the previous equation, we can represent the number of measured fragments as a function of the size of the cut. This is represented in \cref{figs:xs_incoming_estimate_from_fit} right. There, we can see that between $2\sigma$ and $3\sigma$ the number of fragments is constant within $\sim 0.4\%$. However, as the number of $\sigma$ is gets bigger and bigger, we start getting contamination from other isotopes.

\subsection{$N_{frag}^{p2p}$}
\label{xs_number_of_p2p}

Once the number of projectiles has been calculated, the next step is to estimate the total amount of \textsuperscript{24}O measured. Since this nucleus does not have any bound states all \textsuperscript{24}O measured will correspond to the population of the ground state. However, to assert that all of them are produced through QFS p2p, we need to look at the opening angle in CALIFA.

In p2p QFS reactions the interaction is considered as elastic scattering between the proton of the target and the valence proton of the \F. After some kinematical calculations the results is that the opening angle between the outgoing protons has to be centered at $82^\circ$.

This information can be used as a kinematical signature of out p2p reactions, before making any cut in the MDF we will ask for additional conditions. In particular:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Two clusters in CALIFA with energies higher that 20 MeV. The selection of this value is motivated by simulations.
    \item These two clusters have to be coplanar. This means that its $\phi$ coordinate should be almost complementary ($\phi_1 + \phi_2 = 180^\circ \pm 30^\circ$).
\end{itemize}

After applying these two conditions (p2p), we can have a look at the number of \textsuperscript{24}O in the A/Q vs Z plot of the MDF. As we see in \cref{xs_24o_pid}, the blops are better separated than in the unreacted case, so no further correction by contamination will be applied.

To do this, we can make a Gaussian fit to the QFS peak plus a quadratic background and, again, for a given $k_{\text{opa}}$-value, obtain the number of real p2p-QFS events by correcting the ROI-events by both efficiency and contamination. \subsection{$\epsilon_{ToT}$}

Now that we have the raw number for the number of projectiles and the number of \textsuperscript{24}O produced in the target, it's time to account the possible inefficiencies. It's interesting to note that, since we are calculating a ratio between two quantities, all the uncertainties that affect in the same way the number of projectiles and the number of reactions will cancel out, so we are only interested in the differences between the behaviors of these two isotopes.

One very subtle source of uncertainty arises from the energy reconstruction in CALIFA. For individual crystals, energies higher than $\sim 30$ MeV saturate the $\gamma$-range and the time over threshold (ToT) is used to reconstruct the energy according to a exponencial relationship between both quantities:

$$E = A \exp\left(-\frac{ToT}{B}\right)$$

This reconstruction comes with the problem that if the ToT is not present for some events it cannot be used for the reconstruction and the event will be lost. To study this effect, we can use double-range crystals, from CEPA and iPhos, which, in addition to the $\gamma$-range, also have proton range which can be used as a trigger to see if a particles that actually have energy left ToT. So, for this efficieny study, the following scheme will be used:

And, with these number, we can simply use:

$$\epsilon_{\text{ToT}} = 1 - \frac{n_{ToT}^{(rec)}}{n_{ToT}^{(total)}}$$

In \cref{xs_etot_result}, we show the $\epsilon_{ToT}$ efficiency for each crystal of both iPhos and CEPA. Concerning the uncertainties, selecting $k$, independent and equally likely, successes out of $N$ trials is, by definition, a binomial distribution. The best estimator of the probably (i.e., the efficiency) is given by $\hat{p} = k/N$. To estimate its uncertainty, we use the confidence intervals defined in \S40.4.2.3 of \cite{Workman:2022ynf}. As we see in the corresponding picture, there is a dependence in the $\epsilon_{ToT}$ with the part of CALIFA: in iPhos it seems to be relatively constant but there is a more complex structure in CEPA.

\subsection{$\epsilon_{p2p}$}

A very crucial part of the p2p selection is, as explained in \cref{xs_number_of_p2p}, is to detect a QFS signal. These QFS signals are characterized by two coplanar clusters in CALIFA depositing an energy higher than $\sim 20$ MeV. Thus, a clear source of inefficiency, can come from real p2p-QFS events that could not fullfil the conditions of this selection, either for the geometrical acceptance of CALIFA itself or for other effects of the detector.

In order to study this effect we will use the INCL generator to produce \textsuperscript{25}F(p,2p)\textsuperscript{24}O events as source for a realistic GEANT4-based simulation using R3BRoot.

Concerning CALIFA geometry, we have used the 2025 version with a global offset between CALIFA and the target of -4 cm (CALIFA closer to the target). This was fine-tunned so the QFS peaks matched the theoretical $\sim 82^{\circ}$ value. Notice that this value can introduce changes in this p2p efficiency, and its effect will be studied later.

Moreover, the four FOOTs after the target have also been simulated in order provide better energy reconstruction. The complete setup of the simulation is shown in \cref{califa_p2p_sim_setup}.

With respect to the CALIFA digitizer, a very basic setup has been used. For the non-uniformity of the crystals, we have assumed a $1\%$, for the experimental gamma energy resolution, a $6\%$ at 1 MeV has been used and for the detection threshold has been set to $10$ keV. Even though, all the real crystal-by-crystal values are known either from data of this experiment or from past measurements, since we are only interested in seeing if there is a cluster corresponding to a proton or not, the real effect that these minor (and really time-consuming) thing is basically negligible. In order to test our statement, the simulations will be repeated slightly varying these parameters.

On the other hand, a effect that we will do take into account in the digitizer will be the $\epsilon_{ToT}$. As explained in last section, now we know the $\epsilon_{ToT}$ for each crystal: for Barrel crystals we can get it depending on the polar angle of the crystal as $\epsilon_{ToT}$ is known and for iPhos/CEPA this efficiency is 1 since the proton range will be used anyway. So we will use a Metropolis-based algorithm, to study the real efect of  $\epsilon_{ToT}$-inefficiencies in p2p detection:

For each crystal and in each event, we will generate a random number from 0 to 1, if this number happens to be higher than the $\epsilon_{ToT}$ efficiency for the corresponding crystal, the crystal will be discared. After that, the cluster algorithm will be applied only over the valid crytals automatically propagating the effect of the $\epsilon_{ToT}$-inefficiencies in p2p.

Once clusterization is done, with exactly the same algorithm as in experimental data, we will proceed counting, with the same method as described in \cref{xs_number_of_p2p}. Now, by comparing the number obtained in the simulation with the total number of \textsuperscript{24}O generated, we will have access to the product $\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}$.

\subsection{Uncertainties}

Now that we have calculated the values and dependencies of all the quantities necessary to calculate the cross section, the last step is to determinate both its systematic and statistical uncertainties.

\subsubsection{Statistical Uncertainties}

The statistical uncertainties are all the fluctuations in the observables that could be reduced by simply increasing the amount of data.

In our case, since we are not measuring the values $M_t$, $\rho_t$, $N_A$ and $z$, they don't have statistical uncertainties.

Since the $\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{p2p}$ is obtained from a Montecarlo, just by increasing the number of iterations to infinity, its statistical uncertainty will be reduced to zero.

On the other hand, the uncertainty in the measurements of the number of $N_{proj}$ and $N_{frag}^{p2p}$, comes from counting the number of entries inside a window. This is, in first approximation a Poisson-like experiment and, thus, the statistical uncertainty is characterized by $\sqrt{N}$.

Now, assuming that both observables are statistical independent, we can calculate the value of the statistical uncertainty of the cross section by using the standard propagation of errors formula:

$$    \sigma = -\frac{M_t}{\rho_tN_Az}\ln\left(1-\frac{N_{frag}^{p2p}}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}\right)$$

$$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial N_{frag}^{p2p}} = \frac{M_t}{\rho_tN_Az} \cdot \frac{1}{1-\frac{N_{frag}^{p2p}}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}} \cdot \frac{1}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}$$

$$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial N_{proj}} = \frac{M_t}{\rho_tN_Az} \cdot \frac{1}{1-\frac{N_{frag}^{p2p}}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}} \cdot \frac{1}{N_{proj}\epsilon_{p2p}\epsilon_{ToT}\epsilon_{frag}^{rel}}$$
